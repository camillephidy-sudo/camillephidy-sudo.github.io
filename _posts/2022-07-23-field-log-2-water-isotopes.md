## Isotope Water Sampling Initiative

During the expedition's ongoing fieldwork, Senior Researcher Wang Zhigang conducted comprehensive water isotope sampling to reveal water evaporation characteristics across different aquatic environments. This work complements the soil freeze-thaw investigations by examining hydrological linkages to periglacial processes.

## Sampling Locations and Water Sources

Isotope samples were systematically collected from three distinct water bodies:
- **Shahuhe tributary**: Upstream tributary system
- **Datong River**: Major regional water source
- **Thermokarst lake groups**: Periglacial lacustrine environments

## Riverine Wetland Formation Mechanisms

Through field discussion, we documented the formation of a characteristic river-source wetland in the upper reaches of a Shahuhe tributary. The primary mechanism involves freeze-thaw dynamics:

### Formation Process:

1. **Seasonal water accumulation**: During summer monsoon season, high precipitation concentrates at the surface
2. **Subsurface ice melting**: Ground ice beneath the surface melts due to warming, releasing trapped subsurface water
3. **Water convergence**: Surface and subsurface water combine, creating persistent surface moisture accumulation
4. **Wetland development**: Water-logged conditions and associated vegetation patterns form characteristic wetland geomorphology

## Hydrological and Ecological Significance

These river-source wetlands play critical roles in:
- Regulating downstream water availability
- Supporting unique alpine wetland ecosystems
- Providing refuge for specialized flora and fauna
- Acting as carbon storage systems through peat accumulation

## Integration with Freeze-Thaw Studies

Water isotope data will reveal:
- Relative contributions of surface precipitation vs. subsurface ice melt
- Seasonal evaporation patterns across different water bodies
- Water cycle modifications due to climate-driven freeze-thaw intensification
- Linkages between cryospheric processes and ecosystem function

This multidisciplinary approach integrates soil, water, and ecological perspectives to understand Tibetan Plateau system responses to climate change.
