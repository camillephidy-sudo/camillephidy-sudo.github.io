## Mid-Expedition Observations: Diverse Freeze-Thaw Landforms

Approximately one week into fieldwork, during the journey toward Mulin Town, the team observed and documented multiple representative freeze-thaw geomorphological features. Mid-afternoon fieldwork at approximately 3:00 PM revealed a **typical permafrost mound complex** combining features of frost-heave hillocks and early-stage thermokarst lakes.

## Permafrost Mound (Frost-Heave Hillock) Characteristics

Permafrost mounds, locally referred to as "冻胀草丘" (frost-heave tussocks), form through differential ice segregation and ground ice accumulation during frost expansion cycles. Key characteristics include:

- **Ground ice content**: High volumetric ice content in upper soil layers
- **Tussock formation**: Vegetation-covered ice-cored mounds, typically 0.5-2m in height
- **Differential heave**: Non-uniform upward soil displacement due to ice lens formation
- **Spatial patterns**: Regular or semi-regular distribution across periglacial landscapes

## Early-Stage Thermokarst Lake Formation

Adjacent to the frost-heave hillocks, we observed incipient thermokarst lake development, characterized by:
- Shallow surface water accumulation
- Active permafrost degradation around water margins
- Expanding lake boundaries as subsurface ice melts
- Progressive ecosystem transition from terrestrial to lacustrine environments

## Sampling and Aerial Documentation

The team conducted comprehensive soil sampling and aerial photography using unmanned aerial vehicles (UAV) to:
- Map spatial distribution of mound complexes
- Document surface vegetation patterns and stress indicators
- Obtain high-resolution imagery for topographic analysis
- Establish baseline data for future monitoring

## Soil Quality Implications

This mixed periglacial landscape demonstrates varying soil freeze-thaw intensities, enabling investigation of:
- Soil structural changes under differential frost heave
- Microbial community responses to varying ice conditions
- Hydrothermal property variations across the mound-lake continuum
- Carbon cycle modifications in transitional ecosystems
